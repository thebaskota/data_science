Objective of the Exercise
The group should investigate whether there are indications of market manipulation, collusive
behavior, spoofing, or information-driven abuse at the account or market level. Students are not
expected to prove absolute truth, but rather to derive robust probabilities of suspicion from data
patterns.
Data Model and Join Logic
At the core, there are three levels. The market level consists
of markets.csv and market_daily_snapshots.csv. The account level consists
of traders.csv, sessions.csv, transfers.csv, and account_daily_features.csv. The
execution level consists of orders.csv and trades.csv.
Important keys are market_id, trader_id, order_id, and trade_id. orders.csv connects
accounts with markets. trades.csv connects buyers, sellers, and the underlying
orders. sessions.csv and transfers.csv provide infrastructure and money-flow
patterns. account_daily_features.csv already contains pre-aggregated suspicion indicators
on a daily basis and is very suitable for an initial risk score.
Multi-Stage Exercise with 5 Tasks
Task 1: Data Understanding, Data Quality, and Relationship
Model
Students load all CSV files into Pandas, check data types, temporal relationships, and primary
keys, and create a small data model. They should verify
whether order_id, trade_id, market_id, and trader_id are unique, where foreign keys are
referenced, and which fields must be temporally consistent, such as created_at <=
updated_at or session_start <= session_end.
The expected learning outcome is that the group realizes that a fraud case cannot be derived from
a single table. Suspicion only arises through the interaction of market movements, order
behavior, counterparties, technical infrastructure, and money flows.
Concrete assignments:
1. Sketch a join plan.
2. 3. 4. Check for missing values and duplicates.
Convert the most important time fields into real datetime fields.
Mark which fields are likely highly relevant, weakly relevant, or irrelevant noise.

Task 2: Exploratory Analysis of Market and Order Patterns
Students analyze which markets exhibit unusual price and activity patterns. For this, they
combine market_daily_snapshots.csv, orders.csv, and trades.csv. The goal is to identify
markets with strong price movements despite a low number of distinct buyers or sellers, high
cancellation rates, strong concentration on a few accounts, or especially aggressive trades shortly
before close_time.
The expected learning outcome is an understanding of typical manipulation patterns at the
market level. A market is suspicious when price movement and market breadth do not match. A
strong price increase with very few participants or with high cancellation intensity is
significantly more suspicious than the same price movement in a broad, active market.
Concrete assignments:
• Plot price time series per market.
• Compare daily trading activity and price changes.
• Calculate cancel rates per market.
• Analyze the distribution of counterparties.
• Select and justify a top-10 list of suspicious markets.
Task 3: Account Level and Infrastructure Clusters
Students now switch to the account level. They investigate whether multiple accounts are
technically or financially connected. For this, they
use traders.csv, sessions.csv, transfers.csv, and account_daily_features.csv.
Particularly important are shared device_id, identical device_fingerprint_hash, matching
IPs or ASN patterns, high vpn_share or tor_share, and shared withdrawal addresses.
The expected learning outcome is that fraud is often not a single-account problem, but rather
occurs in clusters. Multiple accounts may formally appear different, but use the same technical
infrastructure or the same cashout routes.
Concrete assignments:
• Build a network of accounts if they share devices, fingerprints, IPs, or withdrawal
addresses.
• Then describe cluster sizes, density, and internal trading shares.
• Finally identify the three most suspicious clusters.
Task 4: Heuristic Fraud Score
Students now develop a transparent risk score. The score should not be a black box, but should
consist of clearly interpretable features. Suitable examples include high cancel_count,
low fill_ratio_mean, high top_counterparty_trade_share,
2
high shared_device_session_share, high shared_fp_session_share,
high shared_withdraw_addr_share, high api_order_share, and high self_trade_count.
The expected learning outcome is that good fraud analytics does not begin with complex models.
A well-justified, understandable score is pedagogically more valuable for a seminar than
immediately applying machine learning. The group should justify weights and then examine
which accounts or markets rank highest according to the score.
Concrete assignments:
• Standardize features.
• Document weighting.
• Build an account score and optionally a market score.
• Justify the top cases in written form.
Anyone who wants to may extend the provided starter_analysis.py as a starting point.
Task 5: Interpretation, Counter-Hypotheses, and
Management Summary
In the final step, students write a short interpretation of the results. They should consciously
distinguish between suspicion and proof. For every top case, they must formulate a counter-
hypothesis, such as genuine news, low market liquidity, legitimate API usage, or marketing-
related traffic spikes. Afterwards, they write a short management summary with the three to five
most important suspicious cases and a comprehensible justification.
The expected learning outcome is analytical maturity. A good fraud analysis does not only
identify suspicious patterns, but also acknowledges limitations, uncertainties, and alternative
explanations.
Didactic Note for Execution
A good sequence for the seminar is:
1. First understand the tables.
2. 3. 4. Then calculate simple descriptive statistics.
Afterwards investigate clusters and market patterns.
Only at the end build a composite score.
This teaches students that fraud detection consists of hypothesis generation, data understanding,
and plausibility assessment.

Typical Patterns Students Should Search For
A collusive pattern exists when a few accounts repeatedly trade with each other, use the same
infrastructure, and simultaneously execute an unusually high share of their volume with only one
counterparty.
A spoofing pattern exists when an account places many large orders, allows only minimal
execution, and quickly cancels them again.
An information-driven pattern exists when individual accounts repeatedly position themselves
correctly shortly before market close or before major price movements, while only a few
counterparties are involved.
Small Python Hints for Getting Started
Useful initial metrics are:
• fill_ratio = matched_size_shares / size_shares
• cancel_to_order_ratio = cancel_count / orders_placed
• counterparty_concentration = top_counterparty_trade_share
• shared_infra_score = shared_device_session_share +
shared_fp_session_share + shared_withdraw_addr_share
• near_close_minutes = (close_time - trade_ts).dt.total_seconds() / 60
